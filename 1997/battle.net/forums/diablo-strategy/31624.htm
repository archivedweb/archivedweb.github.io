I agree, for the most part, with your working definition of cheating 
as something is that not an intentional part of the game.  This 
definition is broad enough to include outside/external hacks and 
cheats as well as internal bugs or anomalies, such as duping.  BTW, 
buying things from Wirt for 2/3 of the asking price (back before that 
bug got fixed) also constitutes cheating under this definition (since 
it apparently was not an intentional part of the game).

I understand your desire to see things in black-and-white, but your 
vision got a little "gray" when getting down to cases.  (I 
believe, by the way, that there is a gray area where whether 
something is cheating or not is arguable.)

You mention two examples: backing up and trading.  Backing up is 
using an external program to save multiplayer character data, and is 
not built into the game.  As such, any use of a backup program could 
be considered cheating.  I agree with you, however, that the use to 
which the program is put determines whether it is cheating.  I 
believe that Blizzard didn't put in a character save function because 
of their concern about duplicating items and people avoiding adverse 
events (i.e., losing items) during game play.  Blizzard, in fact, 
seems to be very helpful when people seek to transfer their 
characters to new computers.  Thus, I think that using the backup 
program to restore characters in case of hardware crashes, etc., is 
not cheating.

Trading also cannot be considered cheating, since tradining is 
obviously something Blizzard built into the game and intended to be 
an interactive function of the game.  As long as the items traded are 
legitimate and not duped, it isn't cheating (no matter the relative 
levels of the characters or power of the items).  When you include 
trading beyond 3 levels (which is an arbitrary limit or choice made 
by your clan, and not a game limitation or function) as cheating, you 
go astray.  It may make the game easier for the lower level 
character, but that is their choice, and one provided within the 
game.  The only game limitation on use is the stat requirement (and 
thus, extremely low level characters will be precluded from using 
certain items).  If Blizzard had wanted to restrict the use of 
certain items to certain levels, they would have done so (where have 
I heard this argument before).

If it helps, think of the high level item as a special gift or 
heirloom that the young warrior/mage/rogue learns to use over time 
(if the owner of the powerful item wants to give it up, that's their 
choice).  After all, young Arthur pulled Excalibur from the stone as 
a young man.
