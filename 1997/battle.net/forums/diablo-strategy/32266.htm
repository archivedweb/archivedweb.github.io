If I may elaborate on one thing:

     As a warrior, try to watch your angles.  I know this is 
paramount for a rogue, but it can be useful for the sword-swingers, 
too.  Once a rogue has set up a nice line of fire, don't make her 
move again.  Instead, try to come at a knight from a 90-degree angle 
in relation to the rogue.  Hmmm...maybe I'll try one of these 
diagrams:

XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX           
XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX
R1            W2 M1M2
                .
            .        
     W1

R1=Rogue, W1 equals first position of Warrior, W2 equals second 
position of Warrior, M1 and M2 are monsters

In this case, the Rogue has positioned herself in such a way to give 
her a direct line of fire.  Unfortunately, her warrior friend, in his 
haste to engage with melee monsters, has moved right into her arrow 
stream.  He will either get a few arrows in his back or force the 
rogue to re-position herself if she wants to keep firing at the 
monsters.

Now look at:

XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX
XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX
R1                  M1M2
                      .    W2
                .
       W1

This time the warrior approaches the monsters in such a way as to 1) 
allow the rogue to keep firing continuously, with little chance of 
friendly fire, and 2) allow him to deal out damage to either monster. 
 That means less needles in your back and a happier traveling 
companion.

Well, sorry about the diagrams- I'm really not much of an artist.  
However, hopefully the point will come across:  Warriors, help out 
your rogues and watch how you approach a group of monsters when 
preparing to melee.  This takes some practice, and it certainly isn't 
always easy to do in the heat of battle, but if you can pull it off 
consistently your companions will have a better time adventuring with 
you.
