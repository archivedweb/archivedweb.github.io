Hmmm.... One has to wonder about this one--and also wonder what 
dictionary you're (hehe) using.  Lets take a gander, shall we?

your:  possessive pronominal adj. - of, belonging to, or done by you: 
 also used before some formal titles [your Honor, your Majesty]

you're:  you are

(and for completeness:)  yore:  adj. - long ago; in times long past.  
n. - time long past: now only in "of yore"

So, ask yourself what it is you're (you are) trying to say.  In 
"your a moron"--are you trying to reference the "a 
moron" owned by Tempus?  I doubt it.  If you're trying to imply 
that he/she *is* a moron, then say: "Tempus, you are a 
moron," or, contractionally, "Tempest, you're a moron."  
Now, before you go pointing out the "your Honor" part above, 
note that it is referring to the Honor or Majesty of the person 
addressed (possessive...), and that "your" is appropriate in 
that situation.

Now, I may be totally mistaken, maybe you were referring to a period 
of time, and you should have (should've) used "yore":  yore 
[of] a moron.

So--what was that you're saying about your English?

Grammatically yours,
Chris
