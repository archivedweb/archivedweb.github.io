> The unusual thing about your post, Wiseman, was that the first 
> thought that came to my mind was home.
> 
> Not too many of you probably know too much about Cincinnati, OH, or 
> even more specifically, Clifton.  The University of Cincinnati, where 
> I'm currently going to school, sits right in the middle of Clifton.  
> To sum it up, Clifton is a hole.  I hope I didn't offend anybody.  
> But I walk 4 or 5 city blocks to campus, walking over broken glass, 
> ducking under tree branches, watching out for the part of the 
> sidewalk that has been missing for as long as I've lived here, wave 
> to the colored elderly man that always sits on his porch, nod to the 
> lady on the porch next to his as I look past the Christmas lights 
> still decorating the house, and walk down the hill to campus....  
> 
> ...a campus that is currently just a big wooden wall with bulldozers 
> and whatnot dragging dust everywhere.  The entire campus has about as 
> many trees as I have fingers and toes.  All and all the campus is an 
> eye sore.
> 
> I could write a five hundred page book about the ignorance of UC, 
> from the library that was built with out accounting for the weight of 
> the books to the pastel colored architecture building without a right 
> angle anywhere in it.  But I will not.
> 
> Day in and day out this kind of thing gets old, so I fill the old 
> tank up with gas and embark on my 120 mile treck to the small town of 
> Coldwater, OH every once in a while.  I take old 75 north a ways and 
> turn when I think I'm in the middle of nowhere, and a half an hour 
> later I'm there.  
> 
> Well, what the hell is out there?  Nothing.  It's great.  There is no 
> pollution, no traffic, no maniac psycho triple lane changers, and no 
> crime save the under age drinkers (and we have a bunch of those).  
> Everyone needs to go to these places once in a while, and when these 
> places don't exist anymore, we use our military power capable of 
> blowing up the world twenty times over, and punch out the old time 
> clock.
> 
> And my contribution to all of this will be a drop in an ocean of 
> technology that will eventually turn us all into Gods, or die trying. 
>  Either way, we lose.  I'm not being negative about this, it is just 
> that dying doesn't appear to be too much fun, and having the power to 
> call ourselves God doesn't seem to exciting to me. <yawn>
> 
> I'll tell you this:  In just about everything we do all the fun is 
> getting there.  So whatever ends up happening to us, death or 
> immortality or both, the idea is to get the most out of this crazy 
> parade.
> 
> Now back to my regularly scheduled bit rearrangement....
> 
> Kiri-Jolith
